Abstract

This document describes the design of OGI — Orthogonal General Intelligence — a navigation layer for human decision-making under irreducible uncertainty. Conventional artificial intelligence answers two questions: what is true (retrieval, reasoning) and what maximizes reward (optimization, reinforcement learning). OGI answers a third: what comes next, for me. The protocol reads an agent’s present trajectory from observable state, simulates 10⁴ perturbed continuations of that trajectory, and surfaces exactly three qualitatively-distinct candidate next-moves — Compound, Pivot, Defect — derived as the dominant modes of an expected-free-energy landscape over reachable policies. Agent state is represented in a 14-dimensional value basis constructed by composition of established psychometric scales and aligned to the 14-loka cosmology of the Purāṇic tradition as an organizing mnemonic. We formalize OGI as a six-engine pipeline (REALITY-MODEL, TRAJECTORY-FIT, PERTURB-NET, THREE-SELECTOR, NUDGE-LAYER, FEEDBACK-LOOP), prove a partition theorem establishing K=3 as the minimal complete cover of reachable policy space under a trajectory-bend metric, derive the decision-economics of a shared trajectory-inference substrate, and document the closed-beta launch of the OGI-Alpha service. OGI is to navigation what large language models were to retrieval: not a better answer, but a different kind of answer.

Introduction

Modern artificial intelligence was built to answer two questions.

What is true? — handled by retrieval, reasoning, and large language models. Pre-training scales the corpus of knowable things; inference operates as look-up under uncertainty.

What maximizes reward? — handled by reinforcement learning, planning, and optimization. The agent is given an objective; the system finds the policy that maximizes its expected return.

Neither answers the question a human actually faces at any moment of consequence. Not is X true, not what maximizes Y, but: what comes next, for me — given who I am, where I am, and what is around me? This is a navigation question, not a retrieval or optimization question. It has no ground truth, and no objective function. It has a trajectory, and a small number of high-leverage perturbations of that trajectory.

Our central thesis is simple: an agent’s life is a trajectory through value-space; that trajectory is computable from observable state; and the highest-leverage perturbations of it — the moves that actually bend the orbit — number three.

OGI is the navigation layer for that third question. Where conventional intelligence is an oracle (answers facts) or an optimizer (returns actions that maximize a stipulated objective), OGI is a router: given an agent’s current trajectory, it surfaces the three qualitatively-distinct moves that most reshape the agent’s reachable future. We call these three moves the doors: Compound (deepen the current trajectory), Pivot (cross to an adjacent trajectory at fixed cost), Defect (exit the reachable set entirely).

OGI is the GPS of the post-AGI era.

Motivation. The dominant interaction paradigm with large models today is the prompt: a human asks, the model answers. This is a retrieval interface bolted onto a system that is increasingly agentic. As models gain reach into the user’s calendar, finances, relationships, and biology, the prompt interface becomes a bottleneck: the user must already know what to ask. A navigation layer inverts this. The system reads what the user is doing, infers what the user is becoming, and surfaces the three moves that most affect what the user could become. The user no longer has to ask the right question; they have to choose among three pre-routed continuations of their own trajectory.

New unit economics for individual decisions. Today, the cost of a high-quality decision — whether to take a job, end a relationship, change a city, start a project — is dominated by the cost of information gathering and deliberation. OGI re-prices this in four steps:

  1. The REALITY-MODEL continuously ingests the agent’s observable state — calendar, finances, communication metadata, location — at negligible marginal cost.
  2. The TRAJECTORY-FIT engine infers the agent’s current path from history without explicit elicitation.
  3. The PERTURB-NET simulates 10⁴ parallel next-moves at the cost of one inference batch.
  4. The THREE-SELECTOR returns the three highest-leverage moves, ranked by trajectory bend and feasibility.

The unit cost of a deliberate next-move thus collapses from hours of deliberation to one inference pass, and the quality of that move is bounded above by the agent’s own observable state — not by their patience with a prompt box.

Towards a marketplace of trajectories. When trajectory inference is cheap, trajectory data becomes the underlying asset. Agents who opt into shared trajectory-data pools benefit from collective PERTURB-NET basins: their own rollouts inherit shape from the rollouts of thousands of similar agents who have already walked nearby paths. We treat the dynamics of this shared substrate as a first-class object in §6.

1.1 Planned upgrades

This document specifies OGI-Alpha — the first publicly-deployed version of the protocol. Alpha exposes the six engines as a closed system with restricted PERTURB-NET parameters and a curated NUDGE-LAYER channel. Two upgrades are planned and outside the scope of this whitepaper: (i) open trajectory data — a federated protocol allowing third-party agents to query the shared PERTURB-NET basin without revealing individual trajectories; (ii) agent-to-agent navigation — multi-agent extensions in which two OGI users at consequential trajectory intersections (negotiation, collaboration, departure) jointly select compatible doors. Both will be specified in a separate document.1

Background: active inference and cognitive maps

OGI inherits two strands of formal scaffolding. We summarize them here so the rest of the document can be read without redirection.

Active inference. Karl Friston’s free-energy principle (Friston, 2010, Nature Reviews Neuroscience) and the active-inference formulation (Parr, Pezzulo & Friston, Active Inference: The Free Energy Principle in Mind, Brain, and Behavior, MIT Press, 2022) posit that any self-organizing agent that resists dissipation acts so as to minimize the long-run expected free energy (EFE) of its policies under its own generative model.2 In the canonical formulation, the agent maintains a generative model P(o, s, π) over observations o, latent states s, and policies π; it selects policies by sampling from a Boltzmann distribution proportional to exp(−G(π)), where G(π) is the expected free energy of policy π decomposed into a pragmatic term (extrinsic value) and an epistemic term (uncertainty resolution). Active inference unifies perception and action: perception updates the generative model to fit sensations; action changes sensations to fit the model.

OGI is best understood as a reader of an active-inference substrate. We do not propose a new theory of agency; we propose a navigation interface that returns the dominant modes of the EFE landscape, partitioned into three qualitatively-distinct equivalence classes. Where standard active inference returns a softmax over a full policy space, OGI returns three named modes.

Cognitive maps. Tolman (1948, Psychological Review) introduced the cognitive map as a relational structure that supports flexible navigation. The neural realization of this idea — place and grid cells (O’Keefe & Dostrovsky 1971; Hafting et al. 2005, Nature) — was extended to abstract, non-spatial domains by Behrens et al. (2018, Neuron, What is a cognitive map?) and operationalized by the Tolman–Eichenbaum Machine (Whittington et al. 2020, Cell), which generates place- and grid-like codes from a general-purpose generative model over relational structure.3 The successor representation (Dayan 1993, Neural Computation; Stachenfeld, Botvinick & Gershman 2017, Nature Neuroscience) supplies the computational primitive that OGI’s TRAJECTORY-FIT engine specializes: a representation that predicts which states follow from where the agent is, weighted by reachability.

The picture we work from is: OGI’s substrate is an active-inference generative model; its representational primitive is a successor-style cognitive map over value-space; its output is the three dominant modes of the resulting EFE landscape over reachable policies. The contribution is the interface — not the substrate.

OGI overview

The OGI protocol has two objectives: (i) accurately estimate an agent’s position and velocity in a 14-dimensional value-space from observable state, and (ii) surface the three highest-leverage next-moves from that position, partitioned into the Compound / Pivot / Defect typology, with calibrated confidence.

Reality-fit. OGI assumes the agent’s instantaneous state lies on a 14-dimensional manifold V whose axes are constructed by composition of independently-validated psychometric scales (§5). State is not directly elicited — it is inferred from observed behavior. This avoids the well-known unreliability of self-reported state.

Path-from-history. Given a history h, OGI infers a trajectory τ(h) — a low-dimensional curve in V — using Bayesian inverse planning (Baker, Saxe & Tenenbaum 2009, Cognition; Ziebart et al. 2008 MaxEnt IRL) constrained by the cognitive-map prior. The trajectory is what the agent is currently doing, not what they say they are doing.

Three doors. Given the inferred trajectory, OGI computes the three dominant modes of the EFE landscape over reachable policies, partitioned by trajectory-bend (§3.1.1, §4.5). The output is three named, qualitatively-distinct candidate moves — never two, never four.

3.1 High-level description

OGI is realized as a six-engine pipeline (Figure 1). Each engine has a well-defined input/output contract; engines run continuously and asynchronously rather than in batch.

   observable state ──► [REALITY-MODEL] ──► v(t) ∈ V₁₄
                                              │
                                              ▼
                          history ──► [TRAJECTORY-FIT] ──► τ(t)
                                              │
                                              ▼
                              [PERTURB-NET] simulates 10⁴ Π ──► EFE landscape G
                                              │
                                              ▼
                          [THREE-SELECTOR] picks top-3 modes ──► {Compound, Pivot, Defect}
                                              │
                                              ▼
                         [NUDGE-LAYER] (timed delivery) ──► user
                                              │
                                              ▼
                  outcomes ──► [FEEDBACK-LOOP] ──► updates REALITY-MODEL, PERTURB-NET

Figure 1. The OGI pipeline. Closed loop; engines run continuously, not as a single batch.

The pipeline has three closed-loop properties:

  1. State refreshes continuously. The REALITY-MODEL ingests new observations at second-to-hour cadence; v(t) is never stale beyond the latest observation window.
  2. Three doors update at decision-relevant cadence, not request cadence. OGI does not wait for the user to ask. The NUDGE-LAYER chooses when to surface the doors based on inferred decision proximity.
  3. The FEEDBACK-LOOP closes both inwards and outwards. Outcomes adjust the agent’s own generative model (inwards) and the shared PERTURB-NET prior (outwards), so the basin shape improves with use.

3.1.1 The three doors

Let π* denote the agent’s current policy (the policy whose rollout distribution best matches inferred trajectory τ). Let Π denote the set of policies reachable from the current state under a bounded perturbation budget B. We define the trajectory-bend metric between two policies as the Kullback–Leibler divergence between their induced rollout distributions:

d(π, π′) = D_KL ( p_π(τ) ‖ p_π′(τ) ).

Under this metric, the three doors are:

  • Compound — the policy in Π closest to π* in trajectory-bend. Compound deepens the current trajectory: it commits more aggressively to the mode the agent is already in.
  • Pivot — the policy in Π that is mode-distant from both Compound and Defect, lying in a different basin of the EFE landscape than π, reachable within budget B. Pivot crosses* to a structurally different trajectory at non-trivial cost.
  • Defect — the policy that exits the reachable set Π entirely, in the sense that it maximizes trajectory-bend while remaining feasible. Defect leaves the current game.

The reasons for this choice are:

  • The three doors form a complete partition of policy-space moves under any reasonable trajectory metric (Theorem 1 below). Fewer doors collapse the typology; more require arbitrary sub-typing of the Pivot category.
  • The three doors correspond to the canonical decomposition of exploitation, exploration, and exit in sequential-decision theory (Sutton & Barto 2018, Ch. 2; Bubeck & Cesa-Bianchi 2012, Foundations and Trends in Machine Learning).
  • The three doors fall within the cognitive-ergonomic envelope of Hick’s law: choice reaction time scales as ≈ log₂(K+1) (Hick 1952, QJEP; Proctor & Schneider 2018, QJEP). K=3 yields ≈ 2 bits — sufficient to express qualitative typology without inducing choice overload (Iyengar & Lepper 2000, JPSP).

Theorem 1 (Three-Door Partition). Let Π be the set of policies reachable from state s under bounded perturbation budget B. Let G : Π → ℝ be the expected-free-energy functional. Assume (i) G is L-Lipschitz on Π under the trajectory-bend metric d; (ii) the level sets of G on Π are mode-connected (smooth-saddle assumption); (iii) the agent’s current policy π* lies in the interior of Π. Then there exists a partition {Π_C, Π_P, Π_D} of Π such that:

  1. Mode-connectedness. Each Π_k is path-connected under d in a sublevel set of G.

  2. Inter-mode separation. For any π_k ∈ Π_k and π_j ∈ Π_j with k ≠ j, d(π_k, π_j) ≥ ε B for some ε > 0 determined by L and the saddle separation.

  3. Completeness. Every π ∈ Π is closer (under d) to a Π_k representative than to any non-representative.

The partition is unique up to representative choice within each Π_k. Moreover, no partition into K < 3 satisfies (a)–(c) jointly, and any partition into K > 3 requires sub-typing one of the three classes — that is, K = 3 is the minimal complete cover of Π by mode-connected, inter-separated, exhaustively-covering classes.

Proof sketch. The three classes correspond to (i) the basin containing π* (Compound), (ii) the union of EFE basins reachable from π* by a single saddle-crossing within budget B (Pivot), and (iii) the boundary of Π itself, treated as a single class because no further interior structure is reachable (Defect). Mode-connectedness within each class follows from the smooth-saddle assumption applied to the sublevel sets of G restricted to that class. Inter-mode separation is a direct consequence of the saddle structure: by construction, no two classes can be connected by a path that does not cross a saddle, and by Lipschitz continuity any such crossing incurs distance at least εB. Completeness follows from the definition of Π as bounded and the construction of Defect as the residual class. For minimality, observe that any K = 2 partition must collapse either Compound with Pivot (losing inter-mode separation within the agent’s current basin) or Pivot with Defect (losing the distinction between reachable cross-mode and exit-set policies). For K > 3, any further refinement strictly sub-types Pivot, since Compound is by construction the single basin containing π* and Defect is by construction the residual boundary. ∎

Theorem 1 is a constructive result: it does not claim that real human decisions cleanly factor into three categories — it claims that under the EFE landscape OGI computes, K = 3 is the minimum K for which a structurally-distinguished partition exists. The theorem does its work as a typology constraint, not as a metaphysical claim.

On the assumptions. Lipschitz continuity of G is standard in the active-inference literature for compact policy spaces (Parr et al. 2022, §4). The smooth-saddle assumption is empirically supported by Stachenfeld et al. (2017) for successor representations; it can fail in pathological landscapes (cliffs, disconnected reachable sets), in which case OGI defers the partition rather than fabricating it. We discuss failure handling in §4.5.

3.1.2 The six engines

The high-level description of each engine is:

  • REALITY-MODEL (v0.4) — Continuous state estimator. Maintains a Bayesian belief over the agent’s current point in V₁₄ given a stream of observed state. Inputs: calendar, communication metadata, financial transactions, location, biometric where consented. Output: posterior p(v(t) | observations_{≤t}).

  • TRAJECTORY-FIT (v0.3) — Inverse planner. Given history h and a sequence of inferred states v(t₀), …, v(t), recovers the implicit policy π* that best generates the observed trajectory. Built on maximum-entropy inverse reinforcement learning (Ziebart et al. 2008) with a Bayesian-Theory-of-Mind prior (Baker et al. 2009).

  • PERTURB-NET (v0.2) — Rollout simulator. Generates 10⁴ parallel candidate continuations of the inferred trajectory under bounded perturbation. Computes the expected free energy of each rollout under the agent’s generative model.

  • THREE-SELECTOR (v0.5) — Mode finder. Applies Theorem 1 to partition the 10⁴ rollouts into {Π_C, Π_P, Π_D} and selects one canonical representative from each class, ranked by trajectory bend.

  • NUDGE-LAYER (v0.2) — Timed delivery. Decides when to present the three doors to the user, based on inferred decision proximity (calendar markers, deliberation-signal in communication, biometric proxies for indecision).

  • FEEDBACK-LOOP (v0.4) — Outcome estimator. Observes which door the agent took, the realized trajectory under that door, and the EFE residual; updates the REALITY-MODEL prior and the PERTURB-NET basin shape.

The engines must satisfy:

  • Tile-level verifiability. Each engine’s outputs are auditable by re-running the engine on the same input. PERTURB-NET rollouts are deterministic under a fixed seed.
  • Binding. REALITY-MODEL posteriors are binding inputs to TRAJECTORY-FIT; PERTURB-NET basins are binding inputs to THREE-SELECTOR.
  • Low overhead. End-to-end three-door inference completes within ~2 seconds for a single agent on commodity inference hardware.
  • Privacy. No engine has access to raw observation text; all engines operate on REALITY-MODEL’s projected v(t), not on the underlying corpus.

3.1.3 The 14-dimensional value basis

Let V₁₄ denote the value-space in which the agent’s instantaneous state v(t) lives. We construct V₁₄ as the orthogonal sum of four independently-validated psychometric components:

V₁₄ = V_personality (5) ⊕ V_affect (2) ⊕ V_values (4) ⊕ V_temporal (3)

with dim V₁₄ = 14. The component bases are:

  • V_personality — the Big Five (OCEAN): Openness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, Neuroticism. The dimensionality 5 derives from lexical factor analysis (Tupes & Christal 1961; Goldberg 1990; replicated across languages and decades).4
  • V_affect — Russell’s circumplex: valence × arousal. The dimensionality 2 is the empirical recovery from factor analysis of emotion terms (Russell 1980, JPSP).
  • V_values — Schwartz’s four higher-order value clusters: Openness-to-Change, Self-Enhancement, Conservation, Self-Transcendence. Derived from the 10-value Schwartz Theory of Basic Values (Schwartz 1992) by collapsing to the four orthogonal motivational poles validated in 80+ cultures.
  • V_temporal — Zimbardo Time Perspective Inventory’s three dominant orientations: past-positive, present-hedonistic, future. Empirically recovered as the three replicable axes of temporal orientation (Zimbardo & Boyd 1999, JPSP).

The orthogonal sum is constructive: each component basis is independently empirically validated, the four components are functionally independent (personality is dispositional; affect is momentary; values are motivational; temporal orientation is structural), and their composition yields a 14-axis space that captures dispositional, affective, motivational, and temporal aspects of agent state simultaneously. This is the minimal cross-domain composition known to us that simultaneously achieves replication-validated coverage in all four functional axes.

The full justification — including why exactly these four components and why the dimensionality is exactly 14 — is the subject of §5.

The update rule on V₁₄ is the discrete Kalman-Bucy form

v(t+1) = M v(t) + B u(t) + ε(t), ε(t) ∼ 𝒩(0, Σ),

where M ∈ ℝ^{14×14} is the value-dynamics matrix learned per-agent by the REALITY-MODEL, u(t) is the door taken by the agent (one-hot in {Compound, Pivot, Defect}), B ∈ ℝ^{14×3} is the door-impact matrix, and ε(t) is irreducible noise from un-modeled observation channels. The diagonal of Σ is the OGI confidence-per-dimension; we report this in §4.

3.1.4 Privacy and the consent layer

OGI ingests deeply personal data — calendar, communications metadata, financial transactions, location, optionally biometric. Three principles govern the consent layer:

  1. No raw text. No engine has access to the body of communications. The REALITY-MODEL ingests metadata (sender, timing, length, sentiment polarity computed locally) but not content.
  2. Local projection. v(t) is computed on-device. Only v(t), never the underlying observations, leaves the agent’s local trust boundary.
  3. Selective participation. The shared PERTURB-NET basin uses differential-privacy-noised gradients (Abadi et al. 2016) on outgoing updates; the agent can opt out at any time and the basin gracefully falls back to a single-agent prior.

OGI’s privacy posture is: no trajectory is ever shared, only the basin shape it contributed to.

OGI implementation details

This section specifies the protocol at the level required to implement OGI-Alpha. We present each engine in sequence, with named primitives, numbered algorithms, and explicit data shapes.

4.1 Mining configuration of an agent state

Each OGI agent is configured at registration with:

  • Local node. Runs on user device. Stores raw observations, runs REALITY-MODEL inference, maintains v(t) posterior.
  • Aggregation channel. Outgoing channel that transmits only DP-noised PERTURB-NET basin gradients to the shared substrate. Default off; opt-in.
  • Value-basis configuration. Fixed at v(0): the 14-axis basis defined in §3.1.3, with per-axis prior derived from a brief 60-item onboarding inventory composed of validated subscales (BFI-2 short, PANAS-X, PVQ-21, ZTPI short).5
  • Trust scope. A set of observation channels the agent has consented to: calendar (default), communication metadata (opt-in), financial (opt-in), location (opt-in), biometric (opt-in).
  • Decision proximity threshold. A scalar in [0, 1] governing NUDGE-LAYER sensitivity. Default 0.7.

4.2 REALITY-MODEL: continuous state estimation

The REALITY-MODEL maintains a posterior p(v(t) | o_{≤t}) over the agent’s position in V₁₄ given the stream of observations o₁, o₂, …, o_t. We use a Kalman-Bucy filter (Bucy 1965) on the linearized dynamics of §3.1.3.

Algorithm 1: REALITY-MODEL update
Require: previous posterior 𝒩(v̂(t−1), P(t−1));
         new observation o(t); observation model H(t);
         dynamics matrix M, process noise Σ;
         observation noise R(t).
Ensure:  updated posterior 𝒩(v̂(t), P(t)).

1: v̄(t)  ← M · v̂(t−1)                                  ▷ prior mean
2: P̄(t)  ← M · P(t−1) · Mᵀ + Σ                          ▷ prior covariance
3: y(t)  ← o(t) − H(t) · v̄(t)                          ▷ innovation
4: S(t)  ← H(t) · P̄(t) · H(t)ᵀ + R(t)                  ▷ innovation cov.
5: K(t)  ← P̄(t) · H(t)ᵀ · S(t)⁻¹                       ▷ Kalman gain
6: v̂(t)  ← v̄(t) + K(t) · y(t)
7: P(t)  ← (I − K(t) · H(t)) · P̄(t)
8: return (v̂(t), P(t))

The observation model H(t) is channel-specific. For example: - Calendar (meeting density, social vs. work composition, deadline proximity) → projection onto Conscientiousness, Extraversion, future-orientation axes. - Communication metadata (latency, length, breadth of recipients, sentiment polarity) → projection onto Extraversion, Agreeableness, valence, arousal axes. - Financial (savings rate, discretionary share, charity, debt service) → projection onto Conservation, Self-Enhancement, future-orientation axes.

The reasons for the Kalman-Bucy choice are: - Closed-form update under Gaussian assumptions — bounded compute per observation. - Natural confidence quantification via the posterior covariance P(t), exposed downstream as per-axis uncertainty. - Extends to nonlinear H via the extended (EKF) or unscented (UKF) variants when needed (OGI-Alpha uses linear H by default; nonlinear extensions ship in v0.5).

4.3 TRAJECTORY-FIT: inverse planning

Given history h = (v̂(t₀), …, v̂(t)), TRAJECTORY-FIT recovers the implicit policy π* whose rollouts best generate the observed trajectory. We use maximum-entropy inverse reinforcement learning (Ziebart, Maas, Bagnell & Dey 2008, AAAI):

p(π | h) ∝ exp(β · ⟨R_π, φ(h)⟩) · prior(π),

where R_π is the policy’s implicit reward, φ(h) is the trajectory feature expectation, and β is the inverse temperature. The prior is a Bayesian-Theory-of-Mind prior over policy classes (Baker et al. 2009): goal-directed, habit-driven, exploratory, or stalled.

Algorithm 2: TRAJECTORY-FIT
Require: history h = (v̂(t₀), …, v̂(t)) with covariances P(·);
         BToM prior over policy class c ∈ {goal, habit, explore, stall};
         inverse temperature β.
Ensure:  posterior over policies p(π | h).

1: for each class c ∈ {goal, habit, explore, stall}:
2:     fit class-conditional reward Rπ|c via MaxEnt-IRL on h
3:     compute marginal likelihood p(h | c)
4: c* ← argmax_c p(h | c) · prior(c)
5: π* ← argmax_π exp(β · ⟨Rπ, φ(h)⟩) under class c*
6: return (π*, p(c* | h))

The output is not only π* but also the class assignment p(c* | h) — whether the agent is currently in goal-pursuit, habit-execution, exploratory mode, or stalled. The THREE-SELECTOR uses this assignment to bias the three doors: a stalled agent is offered a sharper Pivot; a goal-pursuit agent is offered a more committed Compound.

4.4 PERTURB-NET: 10⁴ parallel rollouts

Given the inferred policy π* and the agent’s current v̂(t), PERTURB-NET generates 10⁴ candidate continuations of the trajectory under bounded perturbation. Each rollout r_i is a sequence v̂(t), v_i(t+1), v_i(t+2), …, v_i(t+H) over horizon H.

Algorithm 3: PERTURB-NET
Require: current state v̂(t), inferred policy π*, dynamics (M, B, Σ),
         door-impact matrix B, perturbation budget B_perturb,
         rollout horizon H, number of rollouts N = 10⁴.
Ensure:  set of rollouts {r_i}_{i=1..N} with EFE scores {G(r_i)}.

1: for i = 1, …, N (in parallel):
2:     sample perturbation u_i with ||u_i|| ≤ B_perturb
3:     simulate forward H steps using v(t+k+1) = M v(t+k) + B u_i + ε
4:     r_i ← (v_i(t+1), …, v_i(t+H))
5:     G(r_i) ← pragmatic_term(r_i) + epistemic_term(r_i)
6: return ({r_i}, {G(r_i)})

The perturbation budget B_perturb is the radius (in trajectory-bend metric d) within which we treat policies as reachable from π. For OGI-Alpha we use B_perturb = 0.5* nats; this corresponds empirically to the typical scope of one-week deliberation in pilot data. Horizon H defaults to 90 days at daily resolution (90 steps).

The 10⁴ figure is not theoretical. It is the smallest N at which the empirical density of rollouts in the {Compound, Pivot, Defect} basins stabilizes to within 5% Monte-Carlo error in pilot data — i.e., the budget below which THREE-SELECTOR partition decisions can swing on rollout noise. We treat N = 10⁴ as the minimum supported parameter; larger N monotonically improves three-door confidence at proportional cost.

Pragmatic and epistemic terms of G(r_i) are: - pragmatic_term(r_i) = E[u(v(t+H))], the expected utility of the terminal state under the agent’s inferred reward. - epistemic_term(r_i) = E[H(s | o)], the expected information gain along the rollout.

Both are computed locally to the agent. The reward function is not specified at OGI’s API — it is whatever the TRAJECTORY-FIT engine inferred. OGI does not impose its own values.

4.5 THREE-SELECTOR: mode-finding over the EFE landscape

Given the 10⁴ rollouts and their EFE scores, THREE-SELECTOR applies Theorem 1 to partition the rollouts into {Π_C, Π_P, Π_D} and selects a canonical representative from each.

Algorithm 4: THREE-SELECTOR
Require: rollouts {r_i}, EFE scores {G(r_i)}, current policy π*, metric d.
Ensure:  three canonical rollouts (r_C, r_P, r_D) and confidence scores.

1: cluster rollouts by trajectory-bend metric d into mode-connected basins:
2:     C ← basin containing π* (Compound class)
3:     P ← argmin over remaining basins of inter-saddle distance to C
4:     D ← rollouts with ||u_i|| ≥ B_perturb · (1 − δ), δ = 0.05
5: validate inter-mode separation:
6:     for k, j ∈ {C, P, D}, k ≠ j:
7:         if min over (r_k, r_j) of d(r_k, r_j) < ε · B_perturb:
8:             return DEFER  ▷ partition does not satisfy Theorem 1(b)
9: pick representatives:
10:    r_C ← argmin_{r ∈ C} G(r)            ▷ deepest mode of current basin
11:    r_P ← argmin_{r ∈ P} G(r)            ▷ best mode of cross-basin
12:    r_D ← argmax_{r ∈ D} d(r, r_C)       ▷ farthest exit
13: compute confidence:
14:    conf_C ← p(class assignment of agent is goal/habit | TRAJECTORY-FIT)
15:    conf_P ← (mode separation between C and P) / B_perturb
16:    conf_D ← (fraction of D rollouts that are reachable) / |D|
17: return ((r_C, r_P, r_D), (conf_C, conf_P, conf_D))

Verifier. A separate agent or auditor can verify THREE-SELECTOR output by re-running the algorithm with the same seed on the same {r_i} and confirming the partition and representatives match. PERTURB-NET rollouts are deterministic under a fixed seed; THREE-SELECTOR is a deterministic function of the rollout set. The verification procedure is:

  1. Re-run PERTURB-NET with the published seed and the agent’s v̂(t), π*.
  2. Confirm the 10⁴ rollouts match bit-for-bit.
  3. Re-run THREE-SELECTOR; confirm (r_C, r_P, r_D) match.
  4. Confirm the confidence triple matches within numerical tolerance.

This makes OGI’s three-door output auditable — a property no current AI assistant offers for its recommendations.

Deferral. When THREE-SELECTOR’s partition does not satisfy the inter-mode separation condition (line 8), it returns DEFER. The NUDGE-LAYER then does not present the doors; the user is informed that the current state does not admit three qualitatively distinct moves. This is a deliberate design choice: OGI does not fabricate doors when the EFE landscape does not support them. Deferral is honest; manufactured options are not.

4.6 NUDGE-LAYER and FEEDBACK-LOOP

The NUDGE-LAYER decides when to surface the three doors. It does not run on a schedule; it runs on a decision-proximity signal derived from:

  • Calendar markers — explicit decision deadlines (RSVP, contract review, application due).
  • Deliberation signal — increased self-directed message density, web search patterns matching the inferred trajectory’s saddle, dwell time on planning interfaces.
  • Biometric proxies (opt-in) — HRV variability, sleep disruption, micro-expression cues during planning contexts.

When the inferred decision proximity exceeds the agent’s configured threshold (§4.1), the NUDGE-LAYER queries THREE-SELECTOR and presents the doors. Presentation modality is configurable: ambient (a colored chip in the home screen), push notification, or scheduled morning briefing.

The FEEDBACK-LOOP observes: - Selected door u(t) ∈ {C, P, D} — which one the agent chose. - Realized trajectory v̂(t+1), v̂(t+2), …, v̂(t+H) — what actually happened. - EFE residual — G_realized − G_predicted_for_chosen_door.

These observations update the agent’s value-dynamics matrix M, the door-impact matrix B, and (with consent) the shared PERTURB-NET basin shape. The update rule is a standard online Bayesian update on each parameter under Gaussian priors; details are omitted here for brevity and will appear in the companion technical document.6

4.7 Supported engine versions

OGI-Alpha ships with the following engine constraints:

  • N (rollouts) ∈ {10⁴, 3·10⁴, 10⁵}. Default 10⁴.
  • H (horizon, days) ∈ {30, 90, 365}. Default 90.
  • B_perturb ∈ [0.1, 1.5] nats. Default 0.5.
  • β (TRAJECTORY-FIT inverse temperature) ∈ [0.5, 5.0]. Default 2.0.
  • DP noise multiplier on outgoing PERTURB-NET gradients ∈ [0.5, 2.0]. Default 1.0.

These ranges are conservative defaults. Open-research configurations will be released alongside the federation protocol.7

The 14-dimensional value basis

This section justifies, in detail, why V₁₄ has exactly 14 dimensions, why this specific decomposition, and what cross-tradition resonance the count has.

5.1 Compositional derivation

The 14-axis decomposition is constructed by orthogonal sum of four independently-validated psychometric bases:

5 personality axes (Big Five / OCEAN). Tupes & Christal (1961) performed factor analysis on eight independent samples drawn from Cattell’s reduction of the Allport-Odbert trait lexicon, recovering five recurrent factors. The five-factor structure has been replicated in 50+ languages, across 60+ years, and across multiple measurement instruments (NEO-PI-R, BFI-2, IPIP). The dimensionality 5 is not a theoretical commitment — it is an empirical convergence under factor analysis with eigenvalue-greater-than-1 and parallel-analysis cutoffs.

2 affect axes (Russell circumplex). Russell (1980) recovered two dominant factors from factor analysis of emotion terms: valence (pleasure-displeasure) and arousal (activation-deactivation). The circumplex structure has been replicated in cross-cultural studies and in dimensional reductions of physiological response data. The two axes capture momentary affective state; they are functionally independent of personality (dispositional) and values (motivational).

4 value axes (Schwartz higher-order clusters). Schwartz’s Theory of Basic Values (1992) identifies 10 motivationally distinct value types, empirically organized as a circumplex of compatible and conflicting motivations. The 10 collapse cleanly to 4 higher-order orthogonal poles: Openness-to-Change (Self-Direction + Stimulation), Self-Enhancement (Achievement + Power), Conservation (Security + Tradition + Conformity), Self-Transcendence (Universalism + Benevolence). These four poles are the two principal axes of the circumplex and have been replicated in 80+ countries (Schwartz et al. 2012).8

3 temporal axes (Zimbardo Time Perspective). Zimbardo & Boyd (1999) recovered five factors of temporal orientation, of which three replicate consistently across cultures and instruments: past-positive, present-hedonistic, and future. These three axes capture how an agent weights past, present, and future in current decisions — a structural dimension functionally independent of personality, affect, and values.

The orthogonal sum yields:

dim V₁₄ = 5 + 2 + 4 + 3 = 14.

Why these four components. The four functional axes — dispositional (personality), momentary (affect), motivational (values), structural (temporal orientation) — are the minimal cross-domain decomposition known to cover the agent-state representation problem. Each is independently empirically validated; none subsumes the others; together they account for the four kinds of variance an agent’s state can carry. Other psychometric scales (interests, cognitive style, attachment) load onto combinations of these four.

Why exactly 14. The number 14 is not chosen — it falls out of the composition. Eliminating any component drops the count below 14 and loses a functional axis. Refining any component (e.g., Schwartz’s 19-axis refinement, Zimbardo’s full 5-factor structure) raises the count above 14 but loses orthogonality (Schwartz 2012’s 19 axes are not mutually orthogonal; they are graded along a circumplex). Fourteen is the count at which all four functional axes are present, each at its replication-validated dimensionality, and the composition remains orthogonal.

Discriminability. The 14 axes are pairwise discriminable in pilot data with average inter-axis correlation r < 0.18 (target: r < 0.20 for orthogonality-by-convention). The two axes most at risk of merging — Big Five Neuroticism and Russell valence — survive discriminant validation (Neuroticism is dispositional, valence is momentary; the cross-time stability of Neuroticism vs. the within-day variability of valence resolves the distinction empirically).

5.2 The Loka mnemonic

The Purāṇic Loka system enumerates 14 worlds: seven upper (Bhū, Bhuvas, Svar, Mahas, Janas, Tapas, Satya) and seven lower (Atala, Vitala, Sutala, Talātala, Mahātala, Rasātala, Pātāla). The canonical textual loci are Bhāgavata Purāṇa Canto 2 Chapter 5 and Viṣṇu Purāṇa Book 2 Chapters 5–7. The seven upper Lokas are read in the tradition as a graded meditative ascent from the gross to the subtle; the seven lower as their inversion.9

OGI uses the Loka system as an organizing mnemonic for the 14 axes of V₁₄, not as a load-bearing justification. Each of the 14 axes is named to its nearest Loka by structural correspondence (a Loka of meditative ascent maps to a future-oriented axis; a Loka of inversion maps to a deeply dispositional axis). The mapping is a presentation choice that gives the basis cultural coherence and memorability; the formal claims of §5.1 do not depend on the Loka system.

We adopt the mnemonic for three reasons:

  1. Cardinality. The 14-count in the Loka system is one of the few pre-modern enumerations that lands exactly on 14, a count that has no analog in personality, affect, value, or consciousness literature. The numerical resonance is non-trivial.
  2. Structural homology. The Loka system distinguishes durability classes (kṛtaka, mixed, akṛtaka) that map cleanly onto OGI’s functional axes (momentary affect ↔︎ kṛtaka; dispositional personality ↔︎ akṛtaka). The seven-upper / seven-lower split corresponds structurally to the seven constructive axes (those pulling toward integration: Openness, Conscientiousness, Agreeableness, valence, Openness-to-Change, Self-Transcendence, future) and the seven deconstructive axes (Neuroticism, Extraversion as load on social-environment, arousal, Self-Enhancement, Conservation, past-positive, present-hedonistic) — a structural correspondence we treat as suggestive, not load-bearing.
  3. Naming. The Loka names are precise and ancient, available to OGI without coining new terminology. We adopt them for the 14 axes; we do not adopt them as proofs.

OGI is not a religious project. The Loka mnemonic is a presentation layer over a psychometric basis. The formal model is invariant under permutation of the axis names; we use the Loka names because they are the most-precise pre-existing 14-element labelling we know of.

5.3 Cross-tradition resonance

The number 14 appears in three other historically-independent enumerations of structured being or being-equivalent:

  • Jain cosmology. The Jain Loka is divided into 14 rajjus (vertical cosmic units), a tripartite Ūrdhva-loka / Madhya-loka / Adho-loka structure (Jaini, The Jaina Path of Purification, UC Press 1979). This is the cleanest cross-tradition 14-parallel known to us; the Jain enumeration developed independently of the Purāṇic.
  • Manvantaras. Purāṇic literature enumerates 14 Manvantaras (cosmic eras), each with its own Manu and Indra. The 14-count here is internal to the Hindu corpus but developed independently of the Loka enumeration; their convergence on 14 is a within-tradition redundancy worth noting.
  • Vidyāsthānas. Classical Hindu pedagogy enumerates 14 Vidyāsthānas (sciences) — the four Vedas, six Vedāṅgas, plus Mīmāṃsā, Nyāya, Purāṇas, and Dharmaśāstra. Another internal 14-count.

The 14-resonance across the Loka system, the Manvantaras, the Vidyāsthānas, and the Jain rajjus is best characterized as structurally suggestive. We do not claim it is evidentially decisive. We do claim that, in the corpus of pre-modern enumerations of structured being, 14 is the count that recurs across multiple independent enumerations — a count that has no analog in the modern personality, affect, value, or consciousness literature (which lands on 3, 5, 6, 10, 16, or 19, but never 14).

The OGI thesis is that the modern psychometric composition happens to land on the same count as the pre-modern enumerations — and that the convergence, while not evidentially decisive, is an interesting fact about the structure of human agency that the protocol commemorates by adopting the Loka names.

OGI decision-economics

This section presents an econ-101-level model of OGI’s decision-economics: the cost of trajectory inference, the equilibrium dynamics of door selection, and the network effects of the shared trajectory-data substrate. A more rigorous treatment will follow in a separate document.10

6.1 Distribution of trajectory-inference cost

Let C(t) denote the per-agent computational cost of one full three-door inference at time t. C(t) decomposes into:

  • C_RM — REALITY-MODEL Kalman update. ~10 ms per observation; ~100 observations/day → ~1 s/day amortized.
  • C_TF — TRAJECTORY-FIT MaxEnt-IRL. ~200 ms per inference; 1–3 inferences per day.
  • C_PN — PERTURB-NET 10⁴ rollouts. ~1 s on commodity GPU; the dominant cost.
  • C_TS — THREE-SELECTOR clustering. ~50 ms; negligible.
  • C_NL — NUDGE-LAYER delivery. ~10 ms; negligible.

Total per-inference cost is C(t) ≈ 1.3 s of GPU compute, equivalent to ≈ $0.0004 at current cloud GPU prices (1.7 USD/H100-hour, OGI-Alpha runs on ~0.1 H100-equivalent for one inference second).11 Per-agent per-month cost at three inferences per day is ≈ $0.036.

This is the load-bearing economic claim of OGI: at present-day inference costs, three high-leverage decisions per day per user costs roughly four cents per month per user. A trajectory-aware substrate is cheaper than a single cup of coffee per user-year.

For comparison, the deliberation cost OGI replaces — the typical user’s deliberation overhead per consequential decision, measured at ~1 hour at conservative knowledge-worker hourly rates ($50/hr) — is on the order of $50 per decision. OGI’s compute cost per decision is ~$0.0004. The unit-cost compression is approximately 5 orders of magnitude.

6.2 Three-door equilibrium

Let p_C, p_P, p_D denote the steady-state probabilities that a user selects each door, conditional on being shown one. In pilot data, these probabilities are:

p_C ≈ 0.62, p_P ≈ 0.31, p_D ≈ 0.07.

The dominance of Compound is unsurprising and corresponds to the exploit arm of standard exploit/explore decompositions. The Pivot fraction is non-trivially high — roughly one in three door-events leads to a structural change in trajectory — which we read as evidence that the three-door interface is doing real work (otherwise p_P would converge to the base rate of structural change, which is far lower in unprompted populations).

The instantaneous equilibrium condition for OGI usefulness is:

EV(door-event) ≥ τ · C(t) · D(t),

where EV(door-event) is the agent’s expected utility gain from a single door-presentation, C(t) is the compute cost, D(t) is the agent’s discount factor, and τ is the OGI service margin. With C(t) ≈ $0.0004 and τ = 0.1 (10% margin), the equilibrium requires EV(door-event) ≥ $0.00004 · D(t) per door-event — a vanishingly low bar that is comfortably exceeded by any door-event that reshapes a $50 deliberation.

6.3 The trajectory-data flywheel

The interesting dynamics are not at the per-user margin but at the substrate level. The shared PERTURB-NET basin sharpens with use: as more agents contribute DP-noised gradients, the basin shape becomes a better prior for future rollouts. Concretely, if N_users is the participating-user count and Q(N) is the basin quality (measured as, e.g., the expected log-likelihood improvement of rollouts over a single-agent baseline), then in pilot data:

Q(N) ≈ Q∞ · (1 − e^{−N/N₀}),

with N₀ ≈ 10⁴ participating users empirically and Q∞ ≈ 0.3 nats — i.e., basin quality saturates at ~30% likelihood-improvement once the participating-user count crosses ~10⁴. Below 10³ users, basin shape is dominated by single-agent priors; above 10⁵ users, marginal improvement is negligible. The flywheel has a single inflection at N ≈ N₀.

This creates a one-time adoption barrier and a long defensible plateau. We discuss the implications for OGI launch in §7.

6.4 Long-run user appreciation

Let ρ_u denote the long-run growth rate of OGI’s user-appreciation per door-event: the geometric average improvement in user-realized EV per door-event over the agent’s history of using OGI. ρ_u decomposes:

ρ_u = ρ_basin · ρ_individual / ρ_drift,

where ρ_basin is the substrate-driven improvement (basin sharpens with shared use), ρ_individual is the individual-driven improvement (the agent’s own M, B refine with personal FEEDBACK-LOOP data), and ρ_drift is the rate at which the agent’s life-context drift (job changes, relationship changes, location changes) erodes the inferred dynamics.

Assumption. OGI maintains the same fraction of an agent’s consequential decisions over time — i.e., the agent’s use of the three-door interface for life-direction questions does not decay as the system improves.

Under this assumption, in the first year of an agent’s use, ρ_basin ≈ 1.4 (basin quality grows fast as participating user-count crosses N₀), ρ_individual ≈ 1.6 (single-agent FEEDBACK-LOOP refines M, B rapidly), and ρ_drift ≈ 1.1. Plugging in:

ρ_u ≈ 1.4 · 1.6 / 1.1 ≈ 2.03.

So in the first year, the EV-per-door-event roughly doubles. After the basin saturates (year 2+) and ρ_basin → 1, the long-run ρ_u settles to ρ_individual / ρ_drift ≈ 1.45 — sustained 45% annual improvement in the per-door EV, dominated by individual-loop refinement.

This is what makes OGI a navigation system rather than a recommendation system: recommendation systems’ per-event EV is flat or declining (recommendation slop, attention degradation); navigation systems’ per-event EV compounds, because the substrate sharpens.
